# Crstl visual design system

Crstl uses geometry to create order, hierarchy, and clear data relationships. Geometry should feel functional, not ornamental.

Graphics can be image, SVG, or CSS when they represent a real product concept, workflow, integration network, document, transaction, or data state.

Squares and rectangles are a big part of the visual system because they map naturally to documents, transactions, dashboards, and operational workflows.

## Guardrails

- Do not add decorative background grids, blueprint substrates, corner ticks, corner slash marks, ornamental line work, or standalone hexagon markers.
- Do not add geometry just to make a section feel more "Crstl." If the shape does not carry information, structure content, or support interaction, leave it out.
- Hexagons are allowed only when they are the primary content container for a purposeful module, such as an integration/logo network. They should not be added as small decorative badges.
- Edge fades are allowed for overflowing content such as marquees, carousels, and honeycomb fields. Do not use edge fades as generic decoration.
- Prefer quiet cards, rows, borders, and spacing from the design-system tokens over extra visual motifs.

## Here are some design ideas: 

A section of repeating hexagons can be used when each hexagon contains a real logo or icon in an integration network. It can take up half of a section, with edges fading only because the network continues beyond the viewport. Hovering near a hexagon can raise nearby cells. Clicking one changes the text to the left to describe that integration.

Another section will have scattered rectangles representing transactions. As you scroll it will organized themselves into a stack. 

Use boxes to delineate sections when the boxes are actual layout structure or content containers.

You can fade in sections in squares, meaning sections can be made of boxes or rectangles and can fade in in sequence. 

You can have on the left side a track of small boxes, in 2 columns, as you scroll new boxes fade in, building the stack further. At certain break points, two of those small boxes will pop out to the right and have text inside for that section, then as you scroll further it will slide back into the stack and the next one will slide out later.

Another example is a section made from real content boxes. The background is one color, and the boxes are a different color. Then the boxes start from the top left, and move to the bottom right, changing colors in a wave style. Once the section is filled in completely, it ends up being a solid color since the boxes do not have space or border between them.
