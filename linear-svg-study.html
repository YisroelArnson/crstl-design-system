1. Everything starts with the viewBox

viewBox="0 0 265 262" defines the drawing coordinate system. Width and height only decide how large that coordinate system is rendered on the page.

2. They draw with paths, not primitive boxes

Most of the art is <path d="...">. The M command moves the pen, L draws lines, a draws arcs, and z closes the shape. That lets a slab, cube face, and rounded edge live in one path.

3. The polish is mostly stroke discipline

The palette is almost all dark fills, muted gray strokes, and occasional bright strokes. Notice stroke-width="0.5", stroke-linecap="round", and low contrast strokes like #3E3E44.

4. Depth comes from repetition

FIG 0.2 repeats the same shelf edge at different y positions. FIG 0.4 repeats one long bar with shifted coordinates and different vertical lengths. That repetition is easier to author if you design one unit first, then duplicate and offset it.

5. Filters add the soft technical feel

The shadows are SVG filters: feGaussianBlur, feOffset, and feBlend. They are subtle, but they keep dark fills from flattening into the background.

6. FIG 0.3 cheats with overlays

The blinking dots are not part of the cube path. They are three separate 16 by 16 SVG grids, placed absolutely and transformed with a 3D matrix so they sit on cube faces.

How to make one from scratch

  1. Pick a fixed viewBox, usually around 250 to 320 units wide for this style.
  2. Sketch one isometric face as a path: top point, right point, bottom point, left point, close.
  3. Give the face a dark fill and a very thin gray stroke.
  4. Duplicate the face path, offset it, and vary the stroke brightness to create depth.
  5. Add one high-contrast outline path to establish the hero surface.
  6. Add a small SVG filter for shadow, then keep it subtle.
  7. For motion, animate opacity or transform on small child elements, not the whole illustration.
